Compliance & Governance Reviews

Structured audits of existing policies to identify gaps, reduce legal risk, and ensure regulatory alignment.

Regulatory compliance is not a once-off exercise — it is an ongoing obligation that evolves alongside changes in legislation, workforce composition, and organisational structure. Many organisations operate with outdated employment documentation, inconsistent disciplinary procedures, or policies never formally aligned with Botswana’s applicable labour legislation. These gaps create significant legal and reputational exposure.

LegisEdge conducts structured governance and compliance reviews to help organisations systematically assess whether their existing policies, employment contracts, procedures, and institutional practices meet the requirements of the Employment and Labour Relations Act 2025, associated regulations, and applicable governance frameworks. Our reviews are rigorous, independent, and grounded in a working understanding of how labour compliance is enforced in practice.

Following each review, we deliver a clear, implementable findings report with prioritised recommendations — designed to guide your organisation from identified risk toward structured compliance, step by step.

What We Deliver
  • HR policy and documentation audit reports
  • Compliance gap analysis and findings
  • Prioritised remediation recommendations

Who Benefits

Industrial Relations Advisory

Strategic advice on disciplinary hearings, collective bargaining, dispute resolution, and employer-union relations.

Effective industrial relations management is critical to organisational stability, productivity, and long-term operational continuity. The dynamics between employers, employees, and organised labour are complex — shaped by evolving legislation, collective agreements, workplace culture, and institutional power dynamics. Poorly managed industrial relations can escalate rapidly into disputes, litigation, or industrial action that disrupts operations and damages organisational reputation.

LegisEdge supports organisations in navigating these dynamics through structured advisory and practical frameworks grounded in Botswana labour law and best practice. Whether you are managing a disciplinary process, preparing for collective bargaining negotiations, responding to a trade union recognition demand, or seeking to de-escalate an existing dispute — our advisory is direct, practical, and calibrated to the specific operational realities of your organisation.

We provide pre-hearing advisory, procedural guidance, and representation support for disciplinary matters, ensuring processes are fair, defensible, and consistent with applicable legal requirements. For organisations in collective bargaining environments, we provide negotiation strategy support, agreement drafting, and ongoing employer-union relationship management advisory.

Trade Union & Institutional Governance

Constitutional structuring, governance design, and statutory registration support.

Trade unions, professional associations, and other representative bodies occupy a critical role in Botswana’s labour relations landscape. For these institutions to function effectively, advocate credibly on behalf of their members, and comply with their statutory obligations, they require sound governance frameworks — clearly structured constitutions, defined decision-making procedures, and formal statutory registration that confers legal standing and institutional legitimacy.

LegisEdge provides specialised advisory covering the full spectrum of institutional governance for trade unions and representative bodies — from initial constitutional structuring and governance design through to formal statutory registration under Botswana’s applicable labour legislation. Our advisory in this area is grounded in practical experience: we have guided trade unions and sports bodies through the full registration process, and understand both the technical requirements and the institutional dynamics that shape these engagements.

Beyond initial registration, we support established unions and associations with ongoing governance reviews, constitutional amendments, compliance assessments, and advisory on matters of internal governance — helping institutions remain structured, accountable, and legally compliant as they grow and evolve.

    Dismissal & Discipline

    Summary Dismissal During the Notice Period

    By Unami Nthele · Principal Consultant, LegisEdge Consultancy
    #HumanResources#LabourLaw#IndustrialRelations#HRPractice
    Introduction

    A question that arises with some regularity in the management of workplace exits is whether an employer may summarily dismiss an employee who is already serving out a notice period. The short answer is yes — but only under strictly defined circumstances, and in strict compliance with procedural and substantive requirements.

    The notice period is not a period of immunity from discipline. An employee who has tendered their resignation, or whose employment has been terminated on notice, remains an employee for the duration of that notice period. They are bound by the same obligations, subject to the same standards of conduct, and governed by the same policies and procedures that applied throughout their employment. The existence of a notice period does not suspend the employer’s authority to discipline or dismiss.

    The Legal and Contractual Position

    Under Botswana’s employment regulatory framework, an employment contract subsists in full until its lawful termination. Where notice has been given — whether by the employer or the employee — the employment relationship continues throughout that period. Both parties retain their respective rights and obligations.

    This means the employer retains the right to enforce its disciplinary standards. If an employee commits an act of gross misconduct during the notice period — such as theft, dishonesty, wilful insubordination, serious negligence, or a deliberate breach of policy — the employer is not required to simply endure that conduct until the notice period expires. The employer may initiate disciplinary proceedings and, where warranted by the evidence and the sanction, effect a summary dismissal for cause, thereby bringing the employment to an end immediately without payment in lieu of the remaining notice.

    Key Conditions That Must Be Met

    Summary dismissal during a notice period is legally defensible only where the following conditions are met:

    • The misconduct constitutes a dismissible offence. The conduct in question must be sufficiently serious to justify summary dismissal independently of the notice situation. The employer cannot use the notice period as an opportunity to dismiss summarily for conduct that would ordinarily attract a lesser sanction.
    • A fair disciplinary process must be followed. The employee retains the right to a fair hearing. The employer must issue a notice of allegation, afford the employee a reasonable opportunity to respond, and conduct a hearing before a decision is made. Procedural shortcuts are not permissible simply because the employee is already exiting.
    • The sanction must be substantively fair. The decision to dismiss must be proportionate to the offence. The fact that the employee is leaving does not make dismissal automatic — the employer must still assess the nature and gravity of the misconduct.
    • Documentation must be maintained. The employer should document the process carefully: the allegations, the hearing, the outcome, and the reasoning. This is essential should the matter be referred to dispute resolution.
    Practical Implications

    Where an employer effects a summary dismissal during the notice period, the employee loses the entitlement to the remaining notice pay. The dismissal takes effect immediately, and the record of service should reflect the reason for termination as summary dismissal for misconduct, not resignation or termination on notice.

    This distinction has practical consequences. A record of summary dismissal may affect the employee’s reference profile and future employment prospects. For the employer, it creates a basis for withholding outstanding notice pay and potentially pursuing recovery of other financial obligations where applicable.

    However, employers must be cautious not to conflate a desire to exit a difficult employee more quickly with a genuine case of dismissible misconduct. Manufactured or opportunistic disciplinary proceedings initiated purely to cut short a notice period are legally and ethically indefensible, and would likely be overturned at arbitration.

    Resignation Under Notice vs Mutual Separation

    A related scenario worth distinguishing is where the employer, having received a resignation, wishes to release the employee before the end of the notice period. This is legally permissible — but it must be done with the employee’s agreement or through payment in lieu of notice, not through a unilateral summary dismissal dressed up as disciplinary action. Where mutual separation is agreed upon, this should be clearly documented to avoid subsequent disputes about the characterisation of the termination.

    Garden Leave: A Proactive Tool at the Point of Resignation

    Garden leave is a distinct concept and should not be conflated with summary dismissal or disciplinary action. It is most appropriately invoked at the point at which an employee tenders their resignation notice, not as a reactive response to misconduct arising during the notice period.

    Under a garden leave arrangement, the employment contract remains in full force and the employee continues to receive their full salary and contractual benefits throughout the notice period. They are, however, required to stay away from the workplace, removed from client contact, and denied access to confidential systems, data, and business relationships. The notice period runs its contractual course and the exit occurs at the agreed date.

    The purpose of garden leave is protective, not punitive. Employers typically invoke it where a departing employee poses a commercial risk during the notice period, for example where the employee is moving to a direct competitor, holds key client relationships, or has access to sensitive pricing, strategy, or operational information. Placing the employee on garden leave while honouring their contractual entitlements is frequently the most commercially sensible outcome.

    Garden leave provisions must be expressly included in the employment contract. In the absence of a clear contractual right, placing an employee on garden leave unilaterally may expose the employer to a constructive dismissal claim. HR practitioners should ensure garden leave clauses are standard for roles involving access to sensitive commercial information, key client relationships, or competitive intelligence.

    Practical HR Recommendations

    Beyond the legal position, HR practitioners and managers should consider the following when misconduct arises during a notice period:

    • Assess whether immediate termination is truly necessary. The decision to pursue summary dismissal should be driven by the nature and gravity of the misconduct alone. If the same conduct would not have attracted summary dismissal at any other point in the employment, pursuing it during the notice period is legally and ethically questionable. Managing the employee’s access appropriately for the remainder of the notice period is often the more proportionate response.
    • Maintain strict procedural fairness. The temptation to cut procedural corners because the employee is already leaving must be resisted. A defective process creates the same legal exposure during a notice period as at any other point in the employment relationship. Issue a properly particularised charge sheet, allow reasonable preparation time, conduct a fair hearing, and ensure the presiding officer is impartial.
    • Maintain consistency and objectivity. The decision to pursue summary dismissal should be driven by the nature and gravity of the misconduct, not by convenience or a desire to accelerate the exit. If the same conduct would not have attracted summary dismissal at another point in the employment, pursuing it during the notice period is legally and ethically questionable.
    • Assess whether immediate termination is truly necessary. In some cases, the most pragmatic course is to allow the notice period to run while managing the employee's access and responsibilities appropriately. Not every instance of misconduct during a notice period warrants the disruption and legal risk of a summary dismissal process. Apply a proportionality test: does the conduct genuinely require immediate termination, or can it be managed within the remaining notice period?
    • Document every decision and the reasoning behind it. Whether the decision is to pursue summary dismissal, place the employee on garden leave, or manage out the notice period, the rationale should be clearly recorded. This is critical if the matter is subsequently referred to dispute resolution.

    Procedural Fairness

    Procedural Fairness vs Substantive Fairness: Why Both Matter in Every Dismissal

    By Unami Nthele · Principal Consultant, LegisEdge Consultancy
    #HumanResources#LabourLaw#IndustrialRelations#HRPractice
    Introduction

    Among the most enduring misconceptions in workplace discipline is the belief that if the employee clearly did something wrong, the manner in which the dismissal was handled is secondary. This is legally and practically incorrect. In Botswana’s labour relations framework, a dismissal must satisfy two distinct and independent standards: it must be both substantively and procedurally fair. The failure of either one can render a dismissal legally indefensible, regardless of the employer’s intention or the gravity of the conduct.

    What Is Substantive Fairness?

    Substantive fairness concerns the merits of the dismissal — whether there was a valid reason for it, and whether that reason was sufficient to justify the sanction imposed. In a disciplinary context, this means:

    • Did the employee actually commit the act alleged?
    • Is the act a recognised offence under the organisation’s disciplinary code or applicable policy?
    • Is dismissal an appropriate sanction proportionate to the nature and gravity of the offence?
    • Were mitigating and aggravating factors considered before the sanction was determined?

    A dismissal is substantively unfair if the employee did not commit the offence, if the offence does not justify dismissal, or if the employer failed to apply proper consideration to proportionality and circumstance.

    What Is Procedural Fairness?

    Procedural fairness concerns the process by which the dismissal decision was reached — whether the employer followed a fair, structured, and legally compliant procedure before effecting the dismissal. At its core, procedural fairness requires:

    • The employee was given written notice of the allegations against them, in sufficient detail to understand what they are required to answer to.
    • The employee was afforded a reasonable opportunity to prepare their response.
    • A formal hearing was conducted before a decision was made.
    • The employee was permitted to be accompanied at the hearing by a fellow employee, union representative, or other appropriate representative.
    • The presiding officer was impartial — or at least demonstrably fair — in conducting the process.
    • The employee was informed of the outcome and given an opportunity to appeal.
    Common Employer Mistakes

    Both forms of unfairness arise with regularity in the workplace. Some of the most common employer errors include:

    • Pre-determined outcomes. Where the presiding officer has effectively made their decision before the hearing begins — often betrayed by the tone, brevity, or cursory nature of the process — the hearing fails the procedural standard regardless of what is said in it.
    • Vague or insufficient charge sheets. An employee who does not understand the specific allegation against them cannot mount a meaningful defence. A charge must be particularised, not generic.
    • Failure to consider mitigating factors. Length of service, clean disciplinary record, personal circumstances, and remorse are all relevant to sanction. Dismissal without any engagement with these factors is substantively vulnerable.
    • Disproportionate sanctions. Imposing dismissal for a first offence that would ordinarily attract a warning — without compelling justification — will typically fail the substantive test.
    • Denial of representation. Refusing an employee the right to a representative at the hearing is a straightforward procedural failure.
    Why Both Must Coexist

    Substantive and procedural fairness are not interchangeable, and the strength of one does not compensate for the weakness of the other. An employee who committed serious misconduct may still succeed at arbitration if the process was fundamentally flawed. Conversely, an impeccable procedure built around a substantively unjustified dismissal is equally indefensible. Both limbs must be satisfied for a dismissal to withstand scrutiny.

    At LegisEdge Consultancy, we assist organisations in designing and applying disciplinary frameworks that are both procedurally rigorous and substantively sound. We provide pre-hearing advisory, charge sheet preparation support, and presiding officer guidance to help employers manage disciplinary processes with the care and precision that protects the organisation and upholds the dignity of the process.

    • Are the people who chair your disciplinary hearings trained to distinguish procedural from substantive considerations?
    • Are charge sheets always drafted with sufficient specificity to enable a meaningful defence?
    • Is there a structured appeal mechanism, and is it genuinely independent of the original decision?

    Disciplinary Consistency

    Inconsistency in Disciplinary Action: The Silent Risk in Workplace Management

    By Unami Nthele · Principal Consultant, LegisEdge Consultancy
    #HumanResources#LabourLaw#IndustrialRelations#HRPractice
    Introduction

    One of the most powerful and frequently invoked defences available to an employee at arbitration or before a tribunal is the allegation of inconsistency in disciplinary action. The principle is deceptively simple: where two employees commit the same or substantially similar offence, the employer must apply the same sanction to both — or provide a compelling and objective justification for any differential treatment. Where this principle is violated, the fairness of the sanction — even if proportionate in isolation — is fundamentally undermined.

    The Principle of Consistency

    Consistency in discipline does not mean that every disciplinary outcome must be identical in every circumstance. It means that similarly situated employees who commit similar offences must be treated similarly. The comparison is not superficial — it goes to the nature of the offence, the circumstances, the seniority and role of the employee, and the history of prior offences.

    However, where an employer has previously allowed certain conduct to pass without sanction — or has sanctioned it lightly — and then seeks to dismiss a different employee for the same conduct, the inconsistency becomes immediately relevant. The employee facing dismissal is entitled to point to the prior treatment of others as evidence that the employer’s response is disproportionate or selective.

    Risks of Selective Discipline

    Selective or inconsistent discipline creates several distinct risks for the employer:

    • Substantive vulnerability. An arbitrator who finds that the employer dismissed one employee but not another for the same conduct will be far less likely to uphold the dismissal as substantively fair.
    • Allegations of discrimination. Where the inconsistency appears to track protected characteristics — race, gender, union membership, or similar — the employer faces not only an unfair dismissal claim, but a potentially more serious claim of discriminatory treatment.
    • Erosion of workplace trust. Employees observe how discipline is applied. Perceived favouritism or selective enforcement corrodes morale, damages the legitimacy of the disciplinary framework, and encourages disregard for workplace rules.
    • Creating implied norms. Where certain conduct has been repeatedly overlooked or condoned, the employer may inadvertently create an implied norm that such conduct is permissible. Seeking to dismiss for that conduct — after a long period of condonation — carries significant legal risk.
    Impact on Disputes and Tribunal Outcomes

    At arbitration, inconsistency is a standard line of inquiry. Commissioners and tribunal members will frequently ask: how has this employer dealt with similar conduct in the past? What happened to other employees who committed the same offence? Has this conduct previously been condoned?

    Employers who cannot produce clear evidence of consistent application — or who cannot objectively justify any differential treatment — face a significant risk that the dismissal will be found unfair, even where the misconduct itself is not in dispute. This is a painful and preventable outcome.

    Building a Framework for Consistent Discipline

    Consistency does not happen by accident. It requires a structured disciplinary framework that is applied deliberately and monitored over time. Key elements include:

    • A clear, written disciplinary code that specifies offences and their corresponding sanctions.
    • Centralised oversight of disciplinary decisions — particularly at the sanction stage — to prevent line managers from applying different standards in different departments.
    • Maintenance of disciplinary records that allow HR to review how similar cases have previously been handled.
    • Training for presiding officers and line managers on the consistency principle and its implications.

    Inconsistency is one of the most common — and most avoidable — grounds on which employers lose at arbitration. At LegisEdge Consultancy, we work with organisations to design disciplinary frameworks that are applied consistently, documented rigorously, and structured to withstand scrutiny. We also provide pre-hearing advisory that specifically examines whether a proposed sanction is defensible in the context of the organisation’s own precedents.

    • Has your organisation condoned conduct in the past that it is now seeking to discipline? What is the legal exposure?
    • Is there a mechanism for HR to review and approve disciplinary sanctions before they are communicated to the employee?

    Misconduct & Performance

    Misconduct vs Poor Performance: A Critical Distinction That Every HR Practitioner Must Master

    By Unami Nthele · Principal Consultant, LegisEdge Consultancy
    #HumanResources#LabourLaw#IndustrialRelations#HRPractice
    Introduction

    Few errors in workplace management are as consequential — or as frequently made — as the misclassification of poor performance as misconduct, or misconduct as poor performance. These are legally and conceptually distinct categories, and the processes, standards, and outcomes applicable to each are fundamentally different. An employer who conflates the two — whether through ignorance or impatience — creates significant legal and operational risk that is entirely avoidable.

    Defining Misconduct

    Misconduct refers to deliberate or wilful conduct by the employee that is in breach of the organisation’s standards, policies, or the reasonable expectations of the employment relationship. The critical element is intentionality — or at minimum, recklessness. The employee knew, or ought to have known, that their conduct was wrong, and chose to engage in it nonetheless.

    Examples of misconduct include: theft, dishonesty, insubordination, absenteeism without authorisation, harassment of colleagues, wilful damage to property, and serious breaches of safety protocols. The response to misconduct is disciplinary in nature: it involves an investigation, a formal hearing, and a sanction that may range from a warning to summary dismissal, depending on the severity of the conduct.

    Defining Poor Performance

    Poor performance — or incapacity on grounds of poor work performance — refers to a situation where an employee is genuinely unable to meet the required standard of performance, despite being given a reasonable opportunity to do so. The distinction from misconduct is fundamental: poor performance is typically not a matter of choice or intention. The employee is not wilfully underperforming — they are failing to meet a standard that they may lack the skill, capacity, or understanding to achieve.

    The appropriate response to poor performance is not disciplinary, but remedial and supportive: counselling, clearly communicated performance standards, a structured performance improvement plan, targeted support or training, and a reasonable period within which to demonstrate improvement. Only after this process has been genuinely followed — and the employee has still failed to improve — may the employer consider dismissal on grounds of poor performance.

    The Risks of Misclassification

    Where an employer treats poor performance as misconduct, the consequences can be severe:

    • Unfair dismissal exposure. An employee dismissed for misconduct who was in fact simply underperforming has not been afforded the remedial process they were entitled to. The dismissal will typically be found substantively unfair.
    • Failure of the disciplinary process. Where the employee has not genuinely committed an intentional wrong, the disciplinary framework is the wrong vehicle. Charges cannot be properly particularised, and the hearing is likely to be inconclusive or procedurally vulnerable.
    • Reputational and cultural damage. An employee who is disciplined for what is effectively a capacity issue perceives the process as unjust, as will their colleagues. This erodes trust, morale, and confidence in HR’s integrity and competence.

    Conversely, where genuine misconduct is treated as a performance matter, the employer fails to apply appropriate consequences to deliberate wrongdoing — undermining the disciplinary framework, emboldening the offending employee, and signalling to other employees that misconduct carries no real consequences.

    The Appropriate Process for Each

    For misconduct: Suspend (where appropriate), investigate, charge, hearing, sanction, appeal. The process is structured around establishing whether the employee committed the alleged act, and what consequence that act warrants.

    For poor performance: Identify the gap, communicate the standard clearly, provide support and a realistic improvement timeline, document progress, and conduct a formal incapacity review hearing only after the improvement process has been genuinely applied. The process is structured around giving the employee a fair opportunity to meet the standard before any consequential decision is made.

    — LegisEdge Insight

    The misconduct/performance distinction is one of the most important conceptual foundations of sound HR practice. Getting it wrong exposes the organisation to unnecessary legal risk and, more fundamentally, treats employees unfairly. At LegisEdge Consultancy, we assist HR teams and line managers in correctly classifying workplace issues from the outset, ensuring that the right process is applied, the right standard is applied, and the right outcome is reached — in a manner that is both legally defensible and organisationally sound.

    HR Reflection Questions
    • Does your organisation have a clearly documented performance management process that is distinct from your disciplinary process?
    • Are line managers trained to identify whether an employee issue is rooted in intention (misconduct) or incapacity (poor performance)?
    • Before initiating disciplinary action, does HR ask: is this employee choosing not to perform, or are they unable to perform?
    • Is there a structured performance improvement plan template in use across the organisation, with clear timelines and documented support?
